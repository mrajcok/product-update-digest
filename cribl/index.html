Data Lake vs. Data Warehouse: What is the Difference?

This article discusses the evolving landscape of data storage and processing, particularly in the context of AI and machine learning. It highlights that the choice isn't simply between data lakes and data warehouses, but building a comprehensive data architecture that emphasizes data quality, governance, and pipeline control.

  • Data lakes store raw, multi-format data cheaply, ideal for ML, telemetry, observability, and exploratory analysis.
  • Data warehouses store cleaned, structured data optimized for fast BI, reporting, and governed analytics.
  • Lakehouses combine the scale and flexibility of lakes with the performance and governance of warehouses, leading to hybrid architectures.

Telemetry is the hardest case of the AI access problem | Cribl

Telemetry presents unique challenges for AI access, requiring a different approach than traditional business data.

  • Telemetry data is highly fragmented across various sources (SIEMs, APM, cloud providers, edge infrastructure) lacking a central repository.
  • Schema inconsistencies (OCSF, OTLP, ECS, custom formats) require complex normalization, best handled at query time rather than pre-processing.
  • Cribl advocates for an “AI-first architecture” – querying a single federated layer for telemetry data instead of direct integrations with each system to avoid creating disparate views of reality.
  • The core claim is that AI failures are often due to limited data access ("it can't see") and that improving access, through abstraction and normalization, is key to reliable AI-driven insights.

Managing data lake data: best practices for scalability and efficiency | Cribl

Managing Data Lake Data: Best Practices for Scalability and Efficiency

This article details best practices for managing data lakes to avoid them becoming costly and unusable "data swamps," particularly in the context of increasing data volumes from AI and other sources. The core claim is that controlling data before it enters the lake – through filtering, enrichment, masking, and routing – is the most effective approach.

  • Zonal architecture is recommended for organizing data based on refinement level, preserving lineage and enforcing quality.
  • Metadata-first governance is crucial, advocating for creating metadata at ingestion for discoverability and compliance.
  • Storage optimization techniques like columnar formats (Parquet, ORC), partitioning, and compaction are key for performance and cost reduction.
  • Automated lifecycle management (tiered storage, retention policies) is essential for controlling costs as data ages.
  • Cribl Stream and Cribl Lake are presented as solutions, with Stream controlling data upstream (filtering, shaping, routing) and Lake providing flexible, schema-on-read analytics.

AI doesn't fail because it's not smart enough. It fails because it can't see. | Cribl

Cribl highlights a critical issue with AI deployments: lack of real-time, complete data access, leading to confident but incorrect decisions.

  • The core claim is that AI failures stem not from a lack of intelligence, but from “blindness” – operating on stale or incomplete data.
  • Cribl argues that current security approaches (sandboxing, endpoint control) address what agents do, but not what they see, which is the fundamental problem.
  • The emergence of multi-agent systems exacerbates the issue, as flawed outputs from one agent can cascade through the pipeline undetected.
  • Cribl positions its platform as a solution by providing a consistent, normalized retrieval layer to ensure agents operate on the same “version of reality,” focusing on data access before governance or model capabilities.

AI incidents are decision failures. Your postmortem should reflect that. | Cribl

AI incidents are often decision failures, requiring a different postmortem approach than traditional system outages.

  • Cribl advocates extending existing postmortem processes to include reconstructing the “decision environment” of AI-driven outcomes, not just focusing on system failures.
  • Key questions for AI postmortems include: What information did the AI have (and lack)? How was the decision made? Who was accountable? And what controls failed?
  • The blog emphasizes the importance of capturing prompts, instructions, retrieved information, and telemetry available to the AI at decision time.
  • A real-world example illustrates how a failure may not be due to the AI being “wrong”, but rather due to lacking necessary context, visibility, and controls.

The Modal Tax: Why agentic analytics breaks the best-of-breed stack

OcientAIQ™ Unified Data Platform aims to eliminate performance and cost issues when using agentic analytics across disparate data systems – a problem they term the "modal tax".

  • OcientAIQ™ provides a single engine to run relational, geospatial, ML (via OcientML™), graph, vector, and real-time analytics, eliminating the need for agents to hop between different systems and incurring associated costs.
  • The “modal tax” manifests as a token cost (context window size for LLMs), an accuracy tax (loss of context during data transfer), and a latency tax (serialization and network overhead).
  • OcientAIQ™’s Compute Adjacent Storage Architecture™ (CASA) utilizes local NVMe to serve both reads and writes, enabling fast access and reducing the need for data movement.
  • Pricing is fixed per-core and capacity-based, which encourages exploratory behavior from agents without incurring per-action costs, and platform documentation is available at docs.ocient.com.
